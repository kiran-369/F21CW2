The animation below illustrates the gradual rise in global temperatures from 1850 to 2020.
NOAA's 2020 Annual Climate Report states that the combined temperature of land and oceans has risen at a mean pace of 0.13 ¬∞F (0.08 ¬∞C) per decade since 1880.

Nevertheless, the rate of increase has more than doubled since 1981, with an average of 0.18¬∞C (0.32¬∞F) per decade.

According to the 2021 Climate Report by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, unless significant and urgent reductions in greenhouse gas emissions are implemented, there will be temperature increases ranging from 1.5 to 2 ¬∞C, accompanied by other detrimental effects.

How have increased emissions impacted global temperatures?

The rise in temperature is mainly caused by greenhouse gas emissions, specifically the emission of CO2.

More than 50% of the world's CO2 emissions come from just five countries, which include the United States.

J. R. McNeill, an environmental historian, observes in "An Environmental History of the 20th World" that between the late 19th century and the close of the 20th century, the global economy increased 14 times, the world population quadrupled, and energy consumption rose fivefold. These rapid transformations were fueled by the development of artificial fertilizers, medicines, vaccines, and industrialization, which significantly enhanced human efficiency. However, these changes also caused a surge in carbon dioxide and other emissions.

Certain countries bear greater responsibility for emissions, whether currently or historically. As the atmosphere is a "public good," shared by all since everyone breathes, determining how to address emissions is complex. On one hand, countries may not want others polluting their air, but on the other hand, may be hesitant to reduce their own emissions if it negatively impacts their economic growth. Moreover, some countries may believe they have the right to pollute, citing it as a necessary stage in industrial development until a particular GDP level is attained. Regardless of perspective, one fact is evident to all: emissions are escalating.

What are the Challenges facing Cities?

UN Habitat reports that despite occupying less than 2% of the Earth's surface, cities consume 78% of the world's energy and generate over 60% of greenhouse gas emissions, making them significant contributors to climate change.

Cooling Degree Day Increases across major cities:

Cities will face a major challenge in providing cooling as the climate warms. Cooling Degree Days (CDD) is a concept used to estimate the amount of active cooling required to maintain a comfortable indoor temperature. It represents the number of degrees the daily average temperature is above 65¬∞F. Climate projections for 2050 and 2080 predict a significant increase in CDDs, with warmer climates experiencing a much larger impact than moderate climates, as shown in the visual below.

How will we navigate the cooling challenge?

Cooling Exposure: City Matrix

The adoption of cooling systems is expected to increase significantly due to the rise of incomes and urbanization in the developing world, leading to a 33-fold surge in worldwide power consumption for air conditioning by 2100. However, the question remains whether this adoption will be evenly distributed globally. To address this, the concept of Cooling Degree Day exposure considers city populations to reflect the magnitude of the cooling challenge that different cities will face. Additionally, factors such as country income level and climate type must also be taken into account to provide a more realistic outlook on the various factors contributing to the cooling challenge.
